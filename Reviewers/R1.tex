\begin{revcomment}[1.1.1]
	The author replied that ``crowd carriers' route choice, platform's matching/pricing strategy, and the interplay between crowdshipping and dedicated delivery'' are fundamental characteristics in crowdshipping. However, almost all mechanism design problems have matching/pricing strategy and the interplay between agents, which do not make the problem specific to crowdshipping. The only element specific to crowdshipping is the route-choice-based bidding. However, the model (The Winner Determination Problem) does not model the route-choice-based bidding. What all matters is the bundle $s$ in the model. The relationship between bundle and route choice is not clarified.
\end{revcomment}

\begin{revresponse}[1.1.1]
	We agree that the route-choice process must be explicitly modeled. To address this, we have systematically restructured our methodology. As detailed in the revised \added{Section 3.2}, we formally introduce a route-specific Pickup and Delivery Problem (PDP) to evaluate bundles. The bidding cost is now explicitly defined as the minimal detour time derived from the PDP, fully anchoring our mechanism in transportation operations.
\end{revresponse}

\begin{revcomment}[1.1.2]
	Is it possible to model the bidding for bundle based on the route choice? The current model looks like to enumerate all possible bundles, each of which corresponds to a route. In another words, all routes are predetermined, so that the platform simply recommends routes to agents for bidding. Is it possible to bid like this way: let the service provider bid for a cost rate per mile for delivery? And model it into the formulas? 
\end{revcomment}

\begin{revresponse}[1.1.2]
	Yes. By introducing the PDP model in \textbf{Section 3.2}, the relationship is now explicitly governed by route choice. The agent's bid cost is strictly constrained by their own spatial and temporal physical constraints (e.g., origin/destination node visits, time windows for shipments, and maximum detour limits). The routes are not predetermined; rather, the bundle feasibility and minimum detour time are inherently derived from the operational constraints of the carrier's baseline trip.
    Regarding a cost-rate-per-mile bid format: while conceptually viable, interpreting everything as a unit rate effectively reduces the model to a single-parameter mechanism which cannot accurately capture the operational heterogeneity—such as varying spare capacities or asymmetric spatial dependencies—of existing routes driven by diverse crowd carriers. 
\end{revresponse}

\begin{revcomment}[1.2]

Is my understanding right? If yes, it is incorrect that the author claims ``The auctions may not fully capture the non-additive valuation of order bundling by crowd carriers and often assign orders one by one to crowd carriers.'' Almost all combinatorial auction models do not rely on the assumption of ``additive valuation''. This is not a novel contribution of this paper.

\begin{revresponse}[1.2]
	Your understanding is perfectly correct. Non-additive valuation is a standard characteristic of combinatorial auctions, non-exclusive to our work. We have rigorously revised our statements to reflect this, citing foundational literature accurately. Our core algorithmic contribution lies not in non-additivity itself, but in mapping and operationalizing these characteristics for crowdshipping's complex routing nuances via our tailored approximation framework.
\end{revresponse}

\begin{revresponse}[1.3]
	The performance gap in small-scale tests ($735$ vs $1250$) represents rare limit cases aligning with our theoretical bounds; detailed calculations verifying this structural compliance have been added. 

    More importantly, to demonstrate the fairness and practical necessity of the Greedy mechanism, we conducted large-scale scalability analyses matching operational densities (up to 1500 carriers/orders), utilizing exact FedEx standard list rates and OSRM geocoded routing data. As shown in the newly added \added{Section 5.1.2} and \added{Section 5.2.4 (pending simulation finalization)}, the exact VCG model is computationally intractable at this scale. Conversely, our Greedy approximation maintains robust costs and sub-second execution, cementing its viability for real-world platforms.
\end{revresponse}
